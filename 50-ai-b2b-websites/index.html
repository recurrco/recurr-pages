Article briefs — first-level content feedback for Nagendra

The 4 derivative pieces from one research project. Each below has a thesis-from-data, supporting evidence, sites to feature, and remaining gaps. Use as starting points; the cuts and order are yours.

Article 01 — Headline piece

Design patterns: what 50 AI-native B2B sites all do

Pattern essay · 1500-2000 words · meta-piece for the cohort

Thesis from the data: AI-native B2B has already converged. The category is two years old and looks like it's twenty — same hero structure, same nav, same compliance shelf, same pricing logic. The interesting question isn't how is AI-native different from old SaaS. It's why does the category that's supposed to be reinventing software look so much like the software it's replacing?

5 patterns that show up everywhere

  • Benefit-first heroes (82%). "Cut code review time & bugs in half." "Get two hours back every day." Almost no manifesto, no benchmark-led, no visionary copy. The H1 word that matters is what changes for the buyer.
  • Product-led navs (78%). Same five-or-six items: Product / Solutions / Pricing / Customers / Resources / Login. The "audience-led" nav (For Developers / For PMs) only shows up on 5/50.
  • Compliance shelf (76%). SOC 2 + GDPR by default. Even seed-stage companies are signaling enterprise-readiness in 2026. Compliance has eaten "trusted by" as the social proof of choice.
  • Demo-first conversion (54%). AI-native is supposed to be PLG. It isn't. Book-a-demo (19) + Contact-Sales (8) = 27 sites that gate the funnel. Only 13/50 actually let you self-sign-up.
  • Inter font (10/50 use it as body). Plus the "Manrope / Onest / Satoshi" cluster of clean geometric sans serifs. Of 50 sites, exactly zero serif headlines.

3 patterns that break the consensus

  • Webflow > Next.js (17 vs 15). The "AI-native" cohort builds its marketing on no-code more than on the framework its founders cite. The infrastructure story is more about velocity than ideology.
  • Hero copy doesn't say "AI" 64% of the time. They've already moved past needing to say it.
  • Founder-voice is functionally dead in B2B — 5/50, all of them dev-tool / coding-agent companies (Ragas, Coderabbit, Braintrust, 11x, Attio).

Sites to feature as exemplars

  • Lindy — perfect benefit-first hero ("Get two hours back every day") + product UI in hero. Confidence high, missing 17.
  • Coderabbit — dark terminal aesthetic, monospace subhead, embedded code-review UI. Founder-voice, authoritative.
  • Attio — corporate-clean done right. "Customer relationship magic." Hero embeds an interactive AI demo.
  • 11x.ai — the contrarian. Alien/astronaut imagery, "Digital workers, Human results." Visual creative risk vs. category norm.
  • Sarvam AI — geographic-positioning outlier. India-first sovereign AI. Different conversation entirely.
Open question: the data says nav structure has converged, but it doesn't tell us why. Worth one founder/marketer DM each from a corporate-voice site (Glean, Writer, Findem) and a founder-voice site (Coderabbit, Braintrust): "Why this nav and not another?"
Article 02 — Components piece

Best of: headers, footers, hero CTAs, pricing pages

Ranked round-up · 1200-1500 words · screenshot-heavy

Thesis from the data: the components that work hardest aren't the ones founders argue about. Headers are 78% the same. Pricing pages are where the category fights. Hero CTAs are remarkably uniform — 17 sites lead with "Book a demo," 9 with "Try for free."

Header categories worth ranking

  • Bold-on-bold: Reevo (massive all-caps hero), Kombai (neon mint on pure black). Best for category-creator positioning.
  • Demo-in-hero: Attio (Ask Attio chatbot embedded in hero), Fyxer (Gmail UI overlay). Best for "show, don't tell."
  • Mockup-as-proof: Lindy (product UI in hero), Sybill (network diagram), Coframe. Best for visual category newcomers.
  • Brand-credibility: Distyl (institutional partner logos in lieu of CTA), Aaru (EY validation banner). Best for enterprise-skewed sales.

Hero CTA rankings (by volume)

  • Book / Get / Request a demo — 17 sites. The default. ["Book a demo" × 4, "Get a demo" × 3, "Request a demo" × 3 + 7 variants]
  • Try / Start for free — 9 sites. Plus 8 "Sign up." 17 total self-serve invites.
  • Contact Sales / Contact Us — 7 sites. Distyl, Cognition, Sarvam — high-touch enterprise.
  • "Get early access" — 1 site. Wait-list pattern is essentially extinct in this cohort.

Pricing page patterns

  • 14/50 are "talk to us" — no public pricing. Slightly higher than expected for SaaS.
  • 11/50 show 6 pricing tiers — too many. Lutra, Relay, Kombai, Inferless, Sybill, Braintrust, Warmly, Rocketlane, Attio, Clay, Murf.
  • 17/50 toggle annual / monthly. 25/50 have a free tier. 19/50 advertise a free trial.
  • Pricing model split: tier (19) ≈ usage-based (17) ≈ talk-to-us (14). Roughly equal — a thin majority for explicit tiering.

Footer signals

  • Compliance badges in footer = 76% (very high)
  • Newsletter signup in footer = ~30% (not as common as expected)
  • Status page link = ~40% (only data-infra and dev-tools sites do this religiously)
Gap: we didn't crawl pricing-page subpages, only the top of /pricing. For an article ranking the best pricing pages, you'd want to manually review the top 6-tier offenders to see if their tier explanations actually justify the count.
Article 03 — Agent-readability

The AEO audit: how AI-native sites get read by AI

Technical/SEO piece · 1200-1500 words · the most "Recurr Press" angle

Thesis from the data: AI-native B2B is in the middle of a quiet shift from being readable by Googlebot to being readable by GPTBot — but the cohort is split. 46% have an llms.txt; 6% block GPTBot. Half the sites are SPAs that scrapers can't read without JS execution. This is the sub-genre where the category leaders are openly competing for citation share, and the laggards don't yet realize the rules changed.

The 4 hard numbers

  • llms.txt adoption: 46%. Plurality, not table-stakes. Top adopters: design-tooling (100%), marketing-ops (67%), coding-agents (67%), customer-support (67%). Laggards: ops-automation (17%), security (0%).
  • GPTBot blocked: 6%. 47/50 have decided LLM citation traffic is desirable. Compare to media — NYT, WSJ, Guardian all block. The B2B SaaS posture is the opposite.
  • Server-side rendering: 48%. Half the cohort can be read by a no-JS scraper. The other half (SPA) requires headless browser execution to extract the hero copy. This matters for AI-search agents that don't run JS.
  • JSON-LD structured data: ~46%. Organization (12), WebSite (9), SoftwareApplication (5). Surprisingly weak adoption — leaves citation context on the table.

The AEO winners

  • Lindy, Glean, Attio, Lutra, Writer — full stack: SSR + llms.txt + JSON-LD + structured nav. Ready to be read.
  • Lindy is the standout — high confidence, 17 missing fields, llms.txt + robots.txt + structured data + benefit-first hero copy. The most "agent-legible" site in the cohort.

The AEO losers

  • 3 sites block GPTBot. We should call them out (and ask what their reasoning is). Could be a sidebar.
  • SPA-only sites (CodeRabbit, Lindy as SPA, half the cohort): hero text not in initial HTML. AI scrapers without JS can't read them. This is a real performance gap.
  • Ops-automation sub-cat (17% llms.txt adoption) — the laggard. Probably because ops-automation buyers don't search-via-LLM yet.

The framing for the article

"In 2026, the new SEO is being read by ChatGPT. AI-native B2B is split: half the cohort is already optimizing for it. The other half is acting like Google is still the only place buyers look." Use the 46% / 6% / 48% / 46% as the structural numbers.

Gap: we don't have data on whether these llms.txt files are actually well-structured (some may exist but be useless). A v2 pass could fetch + grade each one. ~5 min of additional work.
Article 04 — The composite

The composite median AI-native B2B site (Claude generated)

Generated piece · short essay + a Claude-built mockup · 600-900 words

Thesis from the data: if you wanted to build the most boringly average AI-native B2B website in 2026, here's exactly what it would look like. (And: every site that's actually working is a deviation from this average.)

The composite median

  • Hero: 1-column centered. "Cut [X] in half" or "Get [Y] back" — benefit-first, 6 words, doesn't say "AI." Below it: a product UI screenshot.
  • CTA: "Book a demo" (or "Get started" if PLG). Black or near-black button. "Try for free" as secondary.
  • Nav: Product / Solutions / Pricing / Customers / Resources / Login. Sticky. Traditional bar.
  • Below the fold: Logo cloud (grayscale, 8-12 logos). Then features in a cards-grid (3 or 6). Then testimonials with named headshots. Then a CTA slab. Then footer.
  • Type: Inter or Manrope. Sans-on-sans. H1 between 64-96px.
  • Color: One accent color (hex value: anything but #000000), used on buttons + a heading word. Subtle gradients.
  • Pricing: 3-tier or "talk to us." Annual/monthly toggle. Free trial — credit-card-not-required.
  • Footer: 4 columns. SOC 2 + GDPR badges. © 2026.
  • Tech: Webflow on Cloudflare Pages. SSR. JSON-LD Organization. Has llms.txt. Doesn't block GPTBot. GA4 + HubSpot.

Why this is interesting

The composite is so specific that it's actually a checklist: any site missing 3+ items is either intentionally distinctive (Reevo, Kombai, 11x.ai) or accidentally underbaked. Use it as a "score yourself" tool. Tag it as a Recurr Press shelf-ready resource.

How Claude builds the mockup

Generate an HTML page from the median values. Render it. Drop it in the article as the centerpiece visual. ("This is the website 50 AI-native B2B companies built. Find yourself in it.") Hosted at pages.recurr.co/the-composite-site — an asset on its own.

Gap: we should ask 5-10 of the surveyed companies to comment on the composite — "fairly accurate" / "we deliberately broke this rule" / "we didn't realize we were doing this." That's the column we don't have yet.

Methodology — how this dataset was built

Three-stage pipeline: Playwright extractor (visits 5 pages per site, captures DOM/CSS/headers/JSON-LD/screenshots — no LLM) → Deterministic rules layer (~108 fields filled by pure-Python rules over the bundle — no LLM) → LLM judge (one Anthropic Haiku 4.5 call per site with the hero screenshot + compact text excerpts, fills ~40 subjective fields). All wrapped in a $5 cost gate.

50 sites. 148 columns each. 250 screenshots. Total cost: $0.32. Total wall clock: 24 minutes. The original LLM-agent approach (browser-use driving a full agent through every page) projected $50 and 5 hours and hit a 10K-tokens-per-minute rate-limit wall. Splitting deterministic from judgment dropped cost ~160× with no quality loss on factual fields.

Code at ~/recurrco/projects/top-50-research/pilot_v2/. Reproducible: python pilot_v2/pilot.py --all. Source list locked Apr 28 in companies-final.csv. Schema in research-prompt.md (148 cols, frozen enums).
