03 · The moat

Why MOO survives where other F&B brands stall

  • 100% cold kitchen. No LPG. No exhaust regs. Smaller box. Faster setup. The current LPG shock has hurt every other F&B brand for the last six weeks. We've felt nothing.
  • Subway analogy. Forty years since the last big healthy QSR scaled. Lean format, low op-complexity, repeatable. We're built closer to that template than anyone else in our category.
  • Channel survivability. Profitable at the store level even at 95% aggregator dependency. Anything we layer on (direct retail footfall, embed, canteen, app) is upside, not survival.
  • Category innovation that works. Morning slot was concentration risk. We rebuilt the menu. Risk reduced, no revenue lost.

05 · Strategic partnerships

Where MOO finds its audience — and why retail expansion runs through partners, not landlords

Two years of trial-and-error has given us a clean read on who actually buys. Two clusters dominate:

  • Fitness venues. Gyms, sports centers, badminton courts. Already partnered with several across Bangalore. The fit is consistently strong.
  • Affluent-startup co-working. 25–45, slightly health-conscious, above-median payroll. Urban Vault, Beehive, similar.

Both are inside locations where the right audience already gathers. The retail expansion piggybacks on that. Location partner provides the space and the audience; MOO provides the brand, the kitchen, and a commission share. Goal: 30% of volume from direct retail/footfall at these sites.

Open question for Rohit · the buffer

50% buffer is a seed-stage rule. We're not pure seed.

Rohit's playbook calls for +50% on top — and that's right when you're pre-revenue and survival depends on cash. We're a stable two-year-old business with positive store-level economics. The ₹3.47 Cr above already assumes worst-case opex and marketing — i.e., it doesn't credit any internally-generated cash flow during the 12 months. It builds in conservatism inside the line items, not just on top.

So a 50% buffer on top double-counts conservatism. A 25% or even 10% buffer is more honest for our stage.

Question for you: what buffer is right for a stage that's neither pure seed (we have revenue, ops history, brand) nor true Series A (only 3 stores, sub-₹3 Cr ARR)? Smaller round → less dilution today → cleaner Series A in 12 months. But also — is it under-raising for the rollout risk?

Added · the actual case for the valuation

The valuation premium is bought by what's about to happen, not what's already happened

The comp table makes us look reasonable next to other early-stage F&B brands. The harder question — and the one the multiple really has to defend — is whether MOO is worth ₹40 Cr at this stage given ₹2.7 Cr current ARR. The argument:

This is a stable existing business about to unlock a second business model on top. Retail is not a product extension. It's an additive revenue stream that — uniquely — is also multiplicative for the existing business. Every retail store opens a new direct channel and compounds brand visibility, which lifts cloud-kitchen orders too. The impact compounds with every store opened, not just adds.

Plus: this is the first time we are spending serious money on brand. Two years of operating discipline kept brand spend close to zero. The ₹40 L central brand budget is the first real capital we put behind making MOO a known name. Until now, every order was earned by the product alone. From here, brand starts compounding too.

Both of those are why we believe ₹40 Cr post-money is defensible. Not because the trailing numbers justify it — but because the next 12 months unlock a structurally larger business on top of a structurally stable one.

Added · open concern · for honest discussion

Where I'm uncertain about the ask

₹6 Cr feels like over-raising. A ₹4 Cr round (10–25% buffer) covers the actual capital need. The extra ₹2 Cr is buffer-on-top-of-buffer, and it forces dilution we don't strictly need. Probably the round comes down to ~₹4 Cr.

₹40 Cr post-money sounds large for our stage. The honest read: at ₹2.7 Cr current ARR, 15× is rich. The argument above (additive + multiplicative + first-time brand spend) is what gets us there — but it's a forward argument, not a trailing one. Investors who don't buy the forward thesis will price us closer to ₹20–25 Cr post (8–10× ARR). We need to test the ₹40 Cr number against real investor reactions, not just our own logic.

This is a high-pressure number for us. Two years of bootstrapping built MOO on judicious spend. Going out at any valuation feels like we're being asked to defend something we never had to defend before. Want your read on whether ₹40 Cr is the right anchor or whether we should walk in lower and let the round price up if it gets there.
