Three websites is not three times the marketing. It's one-third of the marketing, paid for three times.

A close-read of the three Zieda web properties — what each is, how they relate, where they conflict, and the actual cost of running them as separate brands. The conclusion is uncomfortable but unambiguous: consolidation is not a nice-to-have. It is the prerequisite for everything else.

02 · The half-done consolidation

The decision to merge has already been made — visually, but not structurally.

The most telling artefact in the entire web audit is on the ZAC homepage. Cosmetic dentistry is already listed as a service line under the aesthetic clinic. That is a soft acknowledgment that the dental brand is being absorbed — without anyone having actually retired the dental site. So today, a customer searching for cosmetic dentistry in Dubai may land on either the ZAC site (which lists it) or the ZDL site (which is supposed to own it). There is no canonical answer to "where does dental live?"

This is what a half-done consolidation looks like. It is the worst of both worlds:

  • a)SEO is split. Two sites compete for the same dental queries, both lose. Google sees duplicate intent and demotes both.
  • b)Customers get confused. Two sites, two booking flows, two phone numbers, two design languages. The brand has two voices for one service.
  • c)The team has to maintain both. Twice the content updates, twice the bug-fix surface, twice the analytics setup — for a service line that can only sit in one place.
  • d)The "decision" never actually got made. Until ZDL is formally redirected into ZAC (or into the unified brand), the half-merge stays half-merged. Indecision is a decision.
The pattern

Half-done consolidations are extremely common in growing service businesses. The reason is human, not technical: retiring a brand feels like retiring a child. The dental brand had a separate launch, a separate identity, a separate set of people who fought for it. Pulling the plug feels like loss. But the cost of not pulling the plug — measured in SEO splits, customer confusion, and ops drag — is much higher than the emotional cost of just doing it.

03 · The compounding tax

What three properties actually cost, beyond the obvious.

The line-item cost of running three websites instead of one is not the real story. Hosting is cheap, design refreshes are once-every-few-years, content production is ~3x but plannable. The real tax is invisible and compounding — and it shows up in the four places below.

Tax 01 — Domain authority can't compound

SEO authority is not multiplicative across three weak domains; it is sub-additive. Three sites at DA 15 each are not "DA 45 of marketing power" — they are roughly DA 18 of effective authority, because backlinks, mentions, and content all dilute across the three. Consolidating into one DA 25–30 site is worth more than scaling three to DA 20.

Tax 02 — Paid CAC is roughly 3x

Every paid channel — Google, Meta, TikTok, Snapchat — is being asked to drive traffic to three separate landing pages. Quality scores are calculated per landing page, not per advertiser. Three weak pages = three lower quality scores = three higher CPCs. Same lead, three times the bid.

Tax 03 — Ops drag is roughly 3x

Three CMSes, three analytics setups, three SEO programs, three content calendars, three design systems. Even if each is "lightweight", the total cognitive load on a small team is enormous. This is the single biggest reason the team feels overwhelmed despite not actually shipping much.

Tax 04 — Brand authority leaks

Press wins (Emirates Woman, L'Officiel) link to one site or another, not to a unified property. Search results show fragmented brand presence. AI search engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) have no canonical entity to attribute to "Zieda" — so they hedge or mis-attribute or skip her entirely.

Three brands at one-third strength each is a worse position than one brand at full strength. Always. In every market. Without exception.

04 · The consolidation playbook

How you actually merge three sites into one without losing anything.

The fear with consolidation is always the same: "will we lose our SEO if we move everything to one domain?" The honest answer is no, if it's done correctly — and yes, traffic dips for 4–8 weeks before it recovers higher than before. This is well-trodden territory. The playbook below is the standard one, used in hundreds of similar migrations.

Step 1 · Pick the canonical domain

One domain becomes the unified property. Likely candidates: zieda.com / zieda.ae / ziedas.com — pending availability check. The chosen domain is the one that will hold all content and authority going forward.

Step 2 · Map every URL

Every page on ZBL, ZAC, ZDL gets mapped to its destination URL on the unified site. No URL is left without a target. This is the spreadsheet that protects all the existing SEO equity.

Step 3 · 301 redirects, page-by-page

Every old URL issues a permanent (301) redirect to its new home. Not a blanket redirect to homepage — page-by-page. This is the difference between a clean migration and an SEO disaster.

Step 4 · Unified IA + design

One sitemap. Lounge, clinic, dental as service categories under the parent brand — not as separate brands. One header, one footer, one design system. Booking flows live on the same surface.

Step 5 · Resubmit + monitor

New sitemap submitted to Google Search Console. Indexation watched daily for the first 4 weeks, weekly thereafter. Crawl errors fixed inside 48 hours. Rankings tracked against pre-migration baselines.

Step 6 · Press + backlink update

Major backlinks (Emirates Woman, L'Officiel, Gulf News, trade press) updated to point to the new canonical URLs. Profile pages on Google Business, Bing Places, Apple Maps, Zomato, etc. updated.

Realistic timeline + risk profile

  • T+0Migration day. All redirects go live. The three old sites stop being separate properties.
  • T+2 weeksIndexation dip. Google re-crawls and re-indexes. Organic traffic typically dips 15–30% during this window. This is normal and expected.
  • T+6 weeksRecovery to baseline. Rankings and traffic return to pre-migration levels.
  • T+12 weeksOutperformance. The unified site begins outperforming the three separate sites combined, because authority is now compounding instead of dividing.
  • T+6 monthsStep change. The unified site is materially ahead of where the three sites would have been on their existing trajectory.
A note on the bolt.host clinic booking

The most urgent fix is the ZAC booking surface. Running a high-revenue clinic's booking on a vibe-coded prototype URL is a meaningful operational risk — these prototype hosts have no SLA, no production guarantees, and no support. This needs to come off bolt.host as part of any migration, not after. The migration is the moment to put it on durable infrastructure.

05 · What we'd build it on

The stack should be boring, fast, and outlive the agency.

The wrong question is "which CMS is the prettiest." The right question is "what will Zieda's team be able to maintain in 18 months without the agency that built it?" The answer is some version of: a headless CMS that the team can use without writing code, on a host that doesn't go down, with content the team owns outright.

CMS

Webflow, Framer, or Payload (self-hosted). All three let non-technical people publish content without breaking the design system. Decision depends on team comfort, budget, and how ambitious the editorial layer is. Strong default: Framer for editorial-heavy magazine model.

Hosting + CDN

Cloudflare or Vercel. Both are battle-tested, fast globally (which matters for a Middle East brand serving multilingual visitors), and cost-effective. Cloudflare also gives DDoS protection and bot management for free — non-trivial for a public-facing aesthetic brand.

Booking layer

Booking lives on the unified site, not as a redirect to Zenoti or Instapracto. Backed by API calls into Zenoti (salon) and Instapracto (clinic) once a slot is confirmed. The customer sees one booking experience; the team sees data flow into the right operational system.

None of this is novel. None of it is risky. None of it requires inventing anything. The technology is the easiest part of this audit. Everything in this section is solvable in 8–12 weeks of focused work, by a team that does this kind of work for a living. The reason it hasn't happened is not difficulty — it is the absence of someone whose job it is to make it happen.
