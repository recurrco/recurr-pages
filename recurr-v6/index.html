02 / Typography
Mono = interface. Serif = content.
Two stacks, hard split. Mono for everything structural (nav, labels, metadata, tables, buttons, footer). Serif for everything editorial (headlines, body text, blockquotes). No sans-serif. No middle ground.

Positioning & Category Design

Why Category Design Is the Only GTM Strategy That Compounds

Pratik Mishra Apr 3, 2026 8 min read
[ Featured image — full viewport width ]

Most startups compete on features. They build comparison tables, highlight integrations, and race to match whatever the market leader shipped last quarter. This is not a strategy. This is a treadmill. The companies that win — the ones that build durable, compounding advantages — do something fundamentally different. They don't compete in existing categories. They create new ones.

The Problem with Feature Competition

When you compete on features, you're playing a game designed by your competitors. Every feature you add is a response to their roadmap, not your customer's needs. The inevitable result is convergence — every product in the category starts to look the same, and the winner is whoever spends the most on sales.1

The goal of category design is not to be better. It is to be different in a way that makes "better" irrelevant.

Category design flips this dynamic. Instead of competing on features within an existing frame, you redefine the frame itself. You change what "good" means. You shift the evaluation criteria so that your product is the only one that fits.

08 / ASCII Layer
Move your mouse. Click anywhere.
Braille character density mapping. 400+ Unicode braille characters create smooth visual gradients driven by wave interference. Mouse position creates a local wave. Clicks spawn expanding ripples. This is the homepage background — alive, reactive, technical.
