01 — Brand

India's first
fitness QSR.

02 — Retrospective

What worked.
What didn't.

Working

  • Category-level data thinking. Revenue per time slot, AOV drivers, SKU performance, discount impact.
  • Clear ownership. Kitchen ops, category, brand, finance — each has an owner.
  • Operational stability. Processes, training, and kitchen operations are scalable.
  • Breaking out of overnight oats. Hot Indian menu is live and resonating. 144 SKUs across a full clean eating menu.
  • Zero paid marketing growth. 1 lakh orders, all earned.

Not working

  • Marketing — two consecutive years. No thesis for what marketing should return. Sporadic wins that didn't compound.
  • Aggregator dependency. 97.5% revenue through Swiggy (56%) and Zomato (42%). Platform take rate ~45%.
  • Zero customer relationships. 1 lakh orders served and we don't know a single customer.
  • The October shock. Turned profitable Jun/Jul. Lost it Oct-Dec. Aggregator volatility + festive season + cold menu in winter.
  • No direction = bad marketing. When the company lacked vision, marketing suffered. Not from lack of effort — from lack of clarity.
02 — Continued

The honest
assessment.

We've proven we can make great food, run kitchens well, and build a brand people love. We turned profitable and then lost it. We know what profitability looks like.

Year 3 isn't hoping for it — it's engineering a return on a stronger foundation, with more channels, better category thinking, and actual customer relationships.

"The biggest cost of not having clarity on where we are headed had to be paid by marketing. Now we have that clarity."

03A — Categories as Marketing Machines

The menu is
the magazine.

We've done a great job building categories internally. None of that thinking reaches the customer. No story. No positioning. No campaign energy. For a brand that says "never be boring," our product marketing has been exactly that.

The aggregator listing is our landing page. We treat it as a media property. The menu is a product page. The photos are hero shots. The descriptions are copy. The bundling is cross-sell.

03A — Campaign Model

Four campaign types.

Menu Projects

The Whole Fruit. DIY Oats. New menus built under MOO — each with a specific angle, audience, and reason to exist. Separate storefronts on aggregators for search visibility.

Recipe Campaigns

DIY Overnight Oats. Influencer-led, content-native. The customer is the creator. Every DIY creation is a piece of content.

Category Campaigns

Don't Skip Breakfast. The India Edit. Cheat Day. End of Day. Each category has a narrative, a moment, a reason to be remembered.

Co-Marketing

Supply 6, Bakery partnerships. Categories co-created with partner brands. Discovery + cost sharing + credibility.

Key metrics: Revenue per time slot (6-8 AM has low competition) and AOV push from ₹302 to ₹330 via bundling and add-ons.

03A — Brand Clarity

One brand.
Ministry of Oats.

We are not building multiple brands. We are building one brand — and that task alone is big enough for a team this size.

The Whole Fruit continues as a menu project — a child of MOO, not an independent brand. A premium, gifting-focused experience designed to not depend on aggregators.

Protein Police is paused indefinitely. It was a coping mechanism born in a tough quarter, not a strategic decision.

The Indian Menu is the growth vector. Ministry of Oats should be the first brand India thinks of when they think of oats. Reimagining Indian palate with oats — this is where demand creation happens.

03B — Channels & Partnerships

The internet is bigger
than two apps.

We've invested 100% of effort into Swiggy and Zomato. Our core capability is receiving orders from the internet. The internet is infinitely bigger than two apps. Our effort elsewhere has been infinitely smaller.

The play: B2B2C. We reach customers through partners — offices, coworking spaces, gyms, fitness studios, apartments. The partner owns the audience. We embed ourselves into their ecosystem.

03B — Embedding Strategy

"MOO at X."

Ministry of Oats showing up where our audience is. Not a separate brand. Not a sub-product. Just MOO — wherever fitness-minded people are.

Each partner gets

  • A branded link (ministryofoats.com/partnername)
  • A custom menu tailored to their audience
  • Fixed time slots for ordering
  • A checkout flow
  • Orders fulfilled from the nearest kitchen

Two-tier model

ModeHow it works
Digital EmbedBranded link, sponsored discount, optional commission. Zero capex for MOO.
Physical EmbedRevenue share (% of sales, uncapped upside). Fixed rent replaced by variable cost.
Partner discovery ↓ Digital embed ("MOO at X" — branded link, custom menu) ↓ Volume proves demand (data, not hope) ↓ Physical embed (lean kitchen inside partner space) ↓ Walk-in + delivery from owned location

03C — Tech & Design Moat

Two product layers.

Outpost Platform — How we run

Internal operations. Universal Ingest Engine. Category, Channels, Stores, Finance. As we scale to 15 stores, the platform keeps us disciplined and data-driven.

ministryofoats.com — How we grow

Consumer nerve centre. Ordering, partner menus (50+ branded links), content, loyalty, merchandise, events. The primary marketing KPI shifts to traffic here.

Brand has no delivery radius. Order fulfilment restrictions don't apply to brand. When we open city #2, the audience is already there. MOO's Instagram isn't a restaurant account — it's a media property.

04 — Cost Philosophy

Frugality is
permanent DNA.

Not a phase. Not because we have to — because it's structural advantage. We deliver a premium experience through design and intellect, not expensive inputs. It never feels cheap. It never feels not well thought out.

Every rupee has a thesis. No spend without a clear expected outcome. The last two years taught us what happens when you spend without knowing what success looks like — you become afraid of spending at all.

03C — Kitchen Design

The lazy kitchen.

We designed a lazy kitchen — and that's a feature. Lazy reduces the scope of error. What can go wrong if it's the laziest kitchen out there? The mistakes are the lowest. We will not move away from this.

But the lazy design must be contained within the kitchen. Channels, marketing, and growth can't be treated with a lazy pair of eyes. We need to take more control.

At scale, every kitchen is: low cost, high efficiency, highly reliable, highly predictable.

05 — Risks

What could go wrong.

Embedded channel doesn't ramp

50 partners in Q1 is aggressive.

Start sales day 1 of April. Build tech in week 1. If 20 partners by May, adjust. Don't abandon the channel.

Seasonality (Oct-Dec)

Third consecutive weak Q3.

Hot Indian menu is the weapon. Embedded channels are habit-driven, less seasonal.

Fundraise doesn't close

Capital for physical embeds delayed.

Business approaching break-even. Can grow slower on internal cash flow. 8 stores instead of 15.

Kitchen capacity

33,750 orders/month may exceed capacity.

Central kitchen planned post-fundraise. New city = new kitchen. Monitor utilisation monthly.

Marketing still doesn't work

Year 3 of failed marketing.

Every spend has a thesis. Monthly review. Kill what doesn't work fast. Now we have the vision — marketing has direction.
