Retro — What Went Wrong

We confused a coping
mechanism with strategy.

When we didn't know where we were headed, we coped. We created new brands to get more aggregator real estate. We built storefronts to game search visibility. We tried structured F&B playbooks — aggregator ads, platform promotions, events. Sporadic wins that didn't compound.

The biggest cost of not having a clear direction was paid by marketing. Two consecutive years. Not because of less effort or less time spent thinking about it. Because the company didn't have a vision. When you don't know who to market to, everything becomes confusing. You splash water in every direction and go nowhere.

We served a lakh orders and we don't know a single customer. No relationship, no data, no way to reach them. 97.5% of revenue flows through Swiggy and Zomato. Platform take rate is ~45%. Monthly orders plateaued at ~6K. We turned profitable in June. Lost it by October — aggregator volatility, festive season, cold menu in winter. Six months later, we're still recovering.

Retro — What Changed

Now we know.

Compared to last year, we can see more clearly where we are headed. Last year we were grappling with existential questions — what are we doing, who are we building for, what does this look like? We built the plane while flying it. Now we know where it's supposed to land.

We've proven we can make great food, run kitchens well, and build a brand people love. We know what profitability looks like — we had it and lost it. Year 3 isn't hoping for it. It's engineering a return on a stronger foundation.

Year 3 — Positioning

India's first
fitness QSR.

Not a breakfast brand. Not a health food brand. Not a cloud kitchen. A fitness brand.

Our most excited customers are people who are mindful about what they eat. They ask which protein we use. They want nutritional macros. They care about ingredients. That is our audience — the India that wants to stay fit, stay young, stay youthful.

This clarity changes everything. Messaging, positioning, where we show up, how we design products, what we market, who we market to. The most amount of benefit from this clarity will be for marketing — the one function that suffered the most from not having it.

Year 3 — Focus

One brand.

Ministry of Oats. That is a task big enough for a team this size.

Protein Police is paused indefinitely. It was a decision made in our lowest quarter. A coping mechanism, not a strategy.

The Whole Fruit continues — but as a menu project inside MOO. A premium, gifting-focused experience. A child of Ministry of Oats, not an independent brand.

The Indian Menu is the growth vector. Ministry of Oats should be the first brand India thinks of when they think of oats. Reimagining Indian palate with oats — we've already seen good signs of it. This is where demand creation happens.

We are not in the demand capture business. We don't sell biryani or sandwiches where demand already exists. We create demand. Building for the long run requires a fundamentally different approach.

Year 3 — Categories

The menu is
the magazine.

We've done a great job building categories internally. None of that thinking reaches the customer. No story, no positioning, no campaign energy.

We don't need to invent marketing campaigns. Our product is what we market. We design everything to be absolutely marketable to a fitness audience. The category, the menu, the recipe — that is the campaign.

The aggregator listing is our landing page. The menu is a product page. Photos are hero shots. Descriptions are copy. Bundling is cross-sell. Time-slot visibility is ad placement. Discount strategy is conversion optimisation. We think like a detailed e-commerce store. Not a cloud kitchen that barely changes its menu.

Year 3 — Channels

The internet is bigger
than two apps.

We've invested 100% of our effort into Swiggy and Zomato. Our core capability is receiving orders from the internet. The internet is infinitely bigger than two apps. Our effort on the rest of the internet has been infinitely smaller.

Embedded kitchens. We know where our audience goes — gyms, fitness studios, offices, coworking spaces. We can set up a kitchen there. Not large standalone outlets. Lean kitchens inside partner spaces where demand is proven digitally first.

Year 3 — Moat

Tech, data, design.

Tell me one food brand in India that can do better than us on these three fronts. The F&B industry also includes Zomato and Swiggy — two stellar IPOs in the last decade, built by companies that brought data, tech, and design to food. That's us. Software people building a food brand.

The lazy kitchen is a feature, not a weakness. Lazy reduces the scope of error. But that lazy design is contained within the kitchen. Channels, marketing, and growth demand aggressive, intentional work.

Brand has no delivery radius. You won't follow a cloud kitchen on Instagram. But you'll follow a great media page. When we open city #2, the brand is already there.
