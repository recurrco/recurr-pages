A working thesis · on the evolution of marketing reporting

Marketing reporting has evolved before. It's about to again.

Spreadsheets gave way to warehouses. Warehouses gave way to dashboards. Each leg solved the last one's pain — and quietly introduced its own. This is an argument about the next leg, and the one problem it has to fix that none of the others did.

01The generation we're in today

Capable — and quietly cracked.

Today's stack is genuinely good. A central data warehouse. A full ETL pipeline feeding it. A transformation layer — dbt and its kin — cleaning and modelling on the way out. Marketers consume from the result. By any measure, it's a leap past where we were.

But look at how marketers actually consume. Not from one place — from three, each doing a job it was never designed for.

02The next generation — posed as a question

Everyone agrees an LLM belongs here. Almost no one agrees on its job.

The next generation will be shaped by language models — that part is settled. What's not settled is the question that actually matters: what do we want the model to do? Three answers are on the table, and they are very different bets.

Fig. 2 — Three things we could ask the model to do
A · Answer questions
Ask the data a question, get an immediate answer back. The chat-on-your-warehouse bet.
B · Simplify the surface
Make the dashboard easier to drive. Smooth the existing consumption layer.
C · Help engineer the setup underneath
Fix the layer the marketer never sees — the one that decides whether A and B are even possible.

Most of the market is building A and B. We think the real leg of evolution is C — and the next two folds are why.

03The deeper problem everyone skipped

Marketing data is not like other data.

There's a reason marketing reporting keeps breaking in ways finance and operations reporting don't. The data itself is a different animal — and every general-purpose stack treats it as if it weren't.

It's mostly third-party performance data. You don't own it. It arrives on someone else's schedule, in someone else's schema, and it changes shape constantly — new channels, new campaigns, new dimensions, every quarter.

And its centre of gravity sits where other data's doesn't. Every reporting system has three layers — observed, planned, executed. For finance and ops, the plan is simple. For marketing, the plan layer is the heaviest thing in the system.

Fig. 3 — The three layers, and where marketing's weight actually sits
Observed
what actually happened — spend, impressions, conversions
Planned marketing's heavy layer
many campaigns, many dimensions, plans that branch in ways finance and ops never face
Executed
what was actually shipped against the plan

The weight is in the centre: the planning surface. Get that right and the surface problems — the Excel, the dashboards, the slides — start to dissolve. But you cannot bolt a planning surface onto a stack that was never engineered for marketing's reality.

04What the next generation looks like

It starts one layer down.

Not a general warehouse with marketing data poured into it. A data architecture built specifically for marketing reporting — one that expects third-party, shape-shifting performance data and treats the plan layer as a first-class citizen. Three parts, each rebuilt for how marketing data actually behaves:

Fig. 4 — A purpose-built marketing data architecture → the unlock
ETL
Ingestion that assumes third-party, constantly-reshaping performance data — not a one-time clean schema.
Transformation
Modelling that treats observed / planned / executed as the core, with the plan layer first-class.
Storage & Semantics
A semantic layer that speaks marketing — campaigns, channels, dimensions — not generic SQL.
