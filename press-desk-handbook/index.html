01 · What Press Desk Is

The editorial workspace for Recurr Press.

Press Desk is one of two surfaces inside Sanity. They serve different jobs.

SurfaceWhereUse it for
Press Deskrecurr.co/press-deskDaily editorial work — write, review, publish
Sanity Studiorecurr.co/studioSite configuration, careers posts, anything outside Press

Most of your time will be in Press Desk. You only need the full Studio for things outside the editorial flow.

What's inside Press Desk

  • My Work — articles assigned to you
  • Articles — All articles, or grouped by theme. This is where published pieces live.
  • Releases — weekly drops that bundle articles for a coordinated publish
  • Newsletter — weekly newsletter documents
  • Signals — the queue of automated signals + a history of what's been posted
  • Library — Themes, Authors, Pillars (the reference data your articles point to)
  • Workflow board — a separate Kanban tool in the left sidebar (Brief → Draft → In Review → Approved)
02 · Roles

Who can do what.

RoleWhat they can do
Editor-in-chiefAnything. Last sign-off on every published piece. Only role that can move work into Approved.
EditorWrite, review, leave comments, move pieces from Brief → Draft → In Review.
External bylineGuest authors whose name appears on the byline. They don't log in. We coordinate over email.

If you're an Editor and you want to ship, hand the piece off to the Editor-in-chief for the Approved → Publish step.

03 · Lifecycle

Every article moves through five states.

Briefidea exists, body empty
Draftwriting in progress
In Reviewreviewer comments
Approvedsigned off
Publishedlive on /press
StateMeaningWho moves it forward
BriefThe idea is captured. Title, slug, excerpt, format are set. Body is empty.Author drags to Draft when they start writing.
DraftBody is being written. TLDR and distribution cuts are being filled.Author drags to In Review when ready.
In ReviewA reviewer reads the piece and leaves comments. Reviewer must be assigned.Reviewer drags back to Draft for revisions, or the Editor-in-chief moves it to Approved.
ApprovedSigned off. Ready to ship.The Publish button in the Studio takes it live and removes it from the Kanban.
PublishedLive on recurr.co/press. No longer on the Kanban.(See section 4.)
Review SLA

For a Friday publish, a piece should be in In Review by Wednesday. The review queue gets cleared mid-week. Pieces dropped into review on Thursday evening rarely make Friday.

How to start a new piece

You usually don't. Briefs are seeded ahead of time, one per piece in the monthly plan. You pick up an existing Brief from the Kanban.

If you really need to start from scratch:

  1. Studio → New document → Press Article
  2. Fill in title, slug, excerpt, pillar, byline (yourself), format, theme (if there's an active month)
  3. Save. A workflow card is auto-created in the Brief column.
  4. Drag yourself to Draft when you start writing.
04 · What Happens After Publish

Where a piece lives once it's live.

The Kanban ends at Approved. The Publish action takes the piece live and removes its card from the board. Published pieces don't disappear — they just live somewhere else.

Where to find published pieces

On the public site
Listed at recurr.co/press, sorted by publish date (newest first). Each article is at recurr.co/press/<slug>.
Inside Press Desk
Press Desk → Articles → All articles. Sorted by publish date. This is the editorial archive — every published piece, plus anything in flight that has a slug.
By month / theme
Press Desk → Articles → By theme. Click a theme to see every article tied to it.
By weekly drop
Press Desk → Releases. Each Release lists the articles it bundles. Status moves Building → Scheduled → Published.

Editing something already published

Open the article in Press Desk, edit, hit Publish again. The site re-validates within 1–2 seconds. You don't need to redeploy.

One thing not to do: change the slug after publish. It breaks every inbound link to the piece. If you really need to, set up a redirect first.

06 · The 5 Pillars

Every article gets exactly one.

Pillars are internal classifications — a way to track what we're writing about. They're not public categories yet. Pick the closest match.

01

Enterprise Software Marketing

The big bucket. B2B software GTM, content marketing, demand gen, product marketing, founder-led marketing. Most early pieces fit here.

02

Positioning & Strategy

How companies decide what they are. Categories, frames, pricing, packaging.

03

Design as Communication

Web, product, decks, visual storytelling treated as language — not decoration.

04

Internet & Media

Distribution, content engines, the LLM era, the shift from search to systems that influence buyers. SEO and AI-search pieces live here.

05

Building & Operating

The work itself. Running platforms, ventures, teams. Operational machinery.

Heuristic

Stuck between two pillars? Pick the one that the article's closing argument serves. A SEO piece that ends with "and here's how to think about your category" is Positioning & Strategy, not Internet & Media.

08 · Themes & Releases

The umbrella, and the bundle.

A Theme is the editorial umbrella for a month. A Release is the bundle for a single week.

Monthly theme

  • One theme per month.
  • Has four weekly sub-themes (W1, W2, W3, W4).
  • Every article in that month aligns to the theme.
  • Articles point at the theme via the theme field on the article.
  • Standalone pieces — ones that don't belong to a monthly arc — leave the field blank.

Weekly Release

  • A Release bundles articles that ship together in the same week.
  • Each Release has its own publish time and a status (Building → Scheduled → Published → Reverted).
  • Used when you want a coordinated "week's drop" feel — three or four pieces going live at once.
  • Optional. Pieces can publish independently without a Release.
May 2026 — current month

Theme: "AI Will Eat SaaS — The Four-Layer Turnover." Sub-themes: W1 Interface · W2 Workflow · W3 Data · W4 Business model. Sixteen briefs are already in Sanity for May, grouped into four weekly Releases.

09 · The Newsletter

How the weekly digest works.

The newsletter is a separate document type from articles. It has its own document, its own editor, and its own publishing channel (Substack).

The structure

  • One newsletter per week.
  • Each newsletter has a body, a week label, and a list of articles it covers.
  • Often bundled into the same Release as that week's articles — so the newsletter and the articles ship together.

Where to write it

Press Desk → Newsletter in the left nav. Each entry is one week's edition. Sorted by week label, newest first.

Where it goes

Substack is the public newsletter channel. The plan is for an automated fan-out worker to pick up Approved newsletter docs and post them to Substack — the wiring is built but not yet deployed. Until that's live, newsletters are written in Sanity and copy-pasted to Substack manually.

What goes in it

Mental models for venture businesses. Not tactical content. The bar: would someone bookmark this and refer back to it six months from now?

Each week's newsletter usually pulls in the Newsletter cut companion field from that week's articles, plus a fresh editorial framing on top.

10 · Signals

The automated content firehose.

A Signal is a small, atomic piece of curated content — a funding announcement, a positioning move, a launch, an industry datapoint. The Signal Engine collects them automatically; we approve, we post.

The lifecycle

StatusMeaning
QueuedThe Engine ingested this signal and gave it a relevance score. Waiting for a human to approve or kill.
ApprovedCleared by an editor. Scheduled for posting.
PostedLive — published on the company X account and added to the website signals page.
KilledRejected. Off the queue. Not deleted, just hidden.

Where to find them

  • Press Desk → Signals → Queue (ready to review) — sorted by relevance × novelty. Daily review pass lives here.
  • Press Desk → Signals → Posted — history of what's gone live.
  • Press Desk → Signals → All signals — everything, including killed.

The two outputs

  1. Twitter / X — @recurrco. Posted signals go out as tweets, automatically.
  2. The signals page — recurr.co/press/signals. A living archive that compounds. Over time it becomes a database of B2B marketing moves — useful as a research surface and an SEO magnet.

When you'd touch a Signal

Mostly during the daily review pass: open the queue, scan the top 10–20, kill anything off, approve what's good. Editing a signal is unusual; usually you just approve or kill.

11 · The Library

Reference data your articles point to.

The Library holds the things that articles reference rather than contain. Editing the Library updates every article that references that item.

ItemWhat it isWhen you edit it
ThemesMonthly editorial themes — the umbrella for a month.Once a month, when setting the next theme.
AuthorsBylines — internal team members and external contributors. Includes name, role, bio, avatar, social handles.When a new author joins, or when an existing author updates their bio / photo.
PillarsThe five editorial pillars. Title, slug, description.Almost never. Pillars are deliberately stable.

An author's profile

Each author entry has a publicProfile toggle. When it's on, the author shows up at recurr.co/press/authors and gets their own page at recurr.co/press/authors/<slug>. When it's off, they're still a usable byline on articles, but they don't appear in the public author directory.

External byline accounts default to off — flip them on case by case once you've decided the author should be publicly listed.

12 · The Image Question

An open gap right now.

Recurr Press doesn't yet have a settled answer to "what does an image look like on this site." It's being closed in a parallel design-system thread.

For now

  • A cover image is recommended. Without one the listing card looks thin, but the article page still works.
  • Inline images are allowed. Add alt text and a caption.
  • Pull-quotes are preferred over decorative inline images.
  • No stock photography.
  • Diagrams: sketch it and ask for it to be rendered in the house style.

Once the design system closes, this section will be replaced with concrete specs — cover dimensions, OG template, illustration style, photography rules.

13 · AI Assist

What it's good at, what it isn't.

Use it for

  • Drafting Twitter / X thread cuts from the body.
  • Drafting LinkedIn cuts from the body.
  • TLDR options — paste body, ask for five under 160 chars.
  • Excerpt rewriting — when the body is final, regenerate the excerpt to match.
  • SEO title variants — five keyword-front-loaded options.

Don't use it for

  • The article body itself. The body is the human voice — that's the brand.
  • Naming pillars or formats. AI gets these wrong; they're fixed enums.
  • Image generation inside the Studio — use the design pipeline.
Note

AI Assist requires the Sanity Growth plan. Until that upgrade lands, AI features may be unavailable in the Studio.

14 · Comments & Review

How feedback flows.

Sanity has document-level comments. The flow:

  • Reviewer opens the doc and reads it. The Comments panel is on the right side of the editor.
  • Comments can be on a field ("this excerpt undersells the piece") or on a selection in the body (highlight a sentence, comment on it).
  • The author resolves comments by editing the piece or replying.
  • Once everything is resolved, the reviewer either drags it forward (Approved) or back (Draft) with a summary comment.
One rule

Don't comment and leave. Always move the piece to the next state when you're done reviewing — either back to Draft, or forward to Approved.

15 · Publish Checklist

Before hitting Publish.

  • Title — final, not a working title
  • Slug — final (it won't change after publish)
  • Excerpt — under 280 characters, makes the click feel earned
  • TLDR — under 160 characters, one sentence
  • Cover image — present, or we accept the listing-card hit
  • Body — proofread end to end
  • Pillar — set
  • Byline or Authors — set
  • Format — set
  • Theme — set if there's an active monthly arc, blank if standalone
  • Distribution: Twitter thread, LinkedIn cut, newsletter cut written
  • SEO title — only if it differs from the editorial title
  • Published At — set to the scheduled time

When everything's green, hit Publish in the upper right. The site re-validates within a couple of seconds via the Sanity webhook.

16 · Common Mistakes

And how to avoid them.

MistakeWhat goes wrongFix
Writing the body before the brief is lockedHalf the piece gets thrown outWrite the angle into the Excerpt first. Lock that. Then write the body.
Publishing without a PillarListing card looks half-empty; the piece is hard to find laterAlways set a Pillar.
Filling both Byline and AuthorsOld/new authorship UI conflictSolo: use Byline. Multi-author: use Authors and leave Byline empty. The site renders Authors if present, otherwise Byline.
Skipping distribution cutsThe piece goes live with no social planWrite the Twitter and LinkedIn cuts during Draft. They're easier to write when the body is fresh.
Editing a published piece's slugBreaks every inbound linkDon't. If you must, set up a redirect first.
Leaving Briefs sittingApproval can't happen on a stubEither fill the body or move it back to backlog. Don't let Briefs decay.
17 · When You're Stuck

Quick answers.

What pillar do I pick?
The closest match. The Editor-in-chief will reassign if needed.
What format do I pick?
Essay if it's opinion-led. Research if it's data-led. Co-authored if there's a second name on it.
What theme do I pick?
Whatever the current month's theme is — or leave blank for a standalone piece.
Cover image?
Recommended but not blocking. See section 12.
AI Assist isn't showing up.
The Sanity Growth plan upgrade is pending. Once it's live, AI features will appear.
My piece won't publish.
Check that Pillar, Byline, and Format are all set — those are required. Then check that you're in Approved state.
I broke something.
Sanity has Document History. Revert from there. If you're really stuck, ping the Editor-in-chief.
