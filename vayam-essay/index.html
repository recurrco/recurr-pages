The grid India built before it built the model.

स्वतंत्र बुद्धिमत्ता · खंड १

Across three states and seven sites, India is quietly assembling 35,000 GPUs into a single sovereign compute fabric. No Indian foundation model exists yet. The grid does. This is the story of building the road before the car.

Sovereign AI · Discovery · 18 min read
By Ananya Bhardwaj · with reporting from Bengaluru, Hyderabad, and Visakhapatnam
Published 12 May 2026 · Updated 14 May 2026 · Fact-checked by Vayam Desk
व vayam
Hyperreal Render · Vayam, May 2026. A rendering of the sovereign GPU cluster at the Visakhapatnam data-center cluster, India's first 12,500-GPU site.

In a low rectangular building on the outskirts of Visakhapatnam, six kilometers from the coast, a thousand cubic feet of refrigerated air moves through 1,200 server racks every minute. The racks hold 12,500 Nvidia H200 GPUs. None of them are training an Indian foundation model. Not yet. The building is two years old. It has been waiting.

Eight hundred kilometers north-west, in a smaller cluster outside Hyderabad, another 7,500 GPUs sit in a similar state of readiness. In Pune, 5,000 more. In Bengaluru, 4,000. By the end of FY27, the Government of India's "Sovereign Compute Mission" — an initiative the Department of Electronics and Information Technology announced quietly in March 2025 and has since refused to brief on — will have 35,000 high-end GPUs distributed across seven Indian sites, networked into a single sovereign compute fabric. By any measure, this is the largest piece of public AI infrastructure built outside the United States and China.

India does not have a competitive Indian foundation model. The country's largest research labs — Sarvam, Krutrim, Yotta — have trained capable but second-tier systems on imported compute. What the country does have, increasingly, is the road. The grid. The land, the cooling, the power supply, the chips. The Sovereign Compute Mission has built the car park before the car.

"We are not building an Indian ChatGPT. We are building the right to refuse one we did not build." — Dr. Aravind Srinivas, founder, Vihaan Compute · in conversation with Vayam

This story is about that bet. About why a country with no model decided to build the largest piece of sovereign AI infrastructure outside the two superpowers. About who is paying for it, who is running it, who is supposed to use it, and what happens if no one does. It is also, in places, about the lights. About how much electricity it takes to keep 35,000 GPUs alive through an Andhra Pradesh summer.

PART 01 · 2,400 WORDS The geography of the bet.

The seven Sovereign Compute Mission sites form a triangle. Visakhapatnam, on the east coast, holds the largest cluster — the one with 12,500 GPUs, the one we will return to. Hyderabad and Bengaluru, both inland and both already dense with private data-center capacity, host the second tier. Pune, Chennai, Mumbai, and a yet-unnamed Greenfield site in Sangareddy district make up the remaining four.

Each site was chosen for a different reason. Visakhapatnam for its existing port access to imported cooling equipment and its coastline electricity grid. Hyderabad for its proximity to two of the country's three semiconductor research centers. Bengaluru for the engineers. Pune for tier-2 cost and the proximity to NTPC's coal supply.

The cooling problem is the one no one wants to talk about. An H200 GPU at full load generates roughly 700 watts of heat. A rack of 32 GPUs at full load is a small space heater. Twelve thousand five hundred of them, networked and training at scale, is a small thermal power station running in reverse — pumping heat out instead of generating it. The Visakhapatnam site uses seawater-assisted closed-loop cooling. It is the only one of the seven sites with the geographic permission to do so.

PART 02 · 1,100 WORDS The grid before the model.

A Western reader will ask: why build the compute before the model is ready to use it? Surely the model is the hard part. The chips are commodity. The answer Indian policymakers gave us, off the record across three interviews, was the same in each case. The compute is the part that cannot be bought back later. If we wait, China will buy it. If we wait, the United States will restrict it. If we wait, the moment passes.

The math here is unforgiving. An H200 GPU costs roughly $32,000 at list price. A rack-ready cluster of 12,500 GPUs — chips, networking, cooling, the building itself — runs to approximately ₹6,200 crore. The Sovereign Compute Mission's total publicly-disclosed budget through FY27 is ₹10,372 crore. Visakhapatnam alone accounts for sixty percent of it. The remaining ₹4,000 crore covers six other sites, fewer chips per site, more shared infrastructure.

PART 03 · 1,900 WORDS Who will actually use it?

The hardest question for the Sovereign Compute Mission is the one its planners are most reluctant to answer. Who is going to use 35,000 GPUs? India's largest AI labs combined have used, across their entire training runs, fewer than 4,000 GPU-equivalents. The largest single training run by an Indian-incorporated company was Sarvam's 7B-parameter Sarvam-1 last December — a run that consumed roughly 800 H100s for 14 days.

The Mission has answered this with three lever-pulls. First, a research-allocation program that gives Indian academic institutions free compute time. Second, a startup-allocation program that subsidizes compute for Indian-incorporated AI labs at one-fifth of cloud market rates. Third, a strategic-use allocation reserved for the defence, healthcare, and language-tech sectors — the part of the program no one will discuss on the record.

What the planners will say, on record, is that the mission is not really about today. The 35,000 GPUs are a five-year option. If India produces a serious foundation model in 2027 or 2028, the compute is there. If it does not, the compute supports the second-best outcome: a country that can host, fine-tune, and run foreign models on sovereign infrastructure. The car park, in other words, works without the car.

PART 04 · 1,600 WORDS The lights.

A fully-loaded 12,500-GPU cluster pulls roughly 14 megawatts of continuous power. The Visakhapatnam site currently draws from the Andhra Pradesh state grid, supplemented by an on-site 8-megawatt solar installation completed in February. The supplementation matters. India's power generation mix is still sixty-eight percent thermal. Every GPU-hour at Visakhapatnam, after solar offset, is, in net carbon terms, a coal-hour at the Krishnapatnam thermal station forty kilometers down the coast.

The Sovereign Compute Mission's stated commitment is to net-zero operation by FY30. The road there runs through three projects: a 200-megawatt dedicated solar-and-storage facility in Anantapur, a green-hydrogen pilot at the Visakhapatnam site itself, and a long-term off-take agreement with the Adani-promoted pumped-hydro station coming online in 2028 at Sileru.

Whether any of this happens on the stated timeline is the second-hardest question. The first is whether India produces a model worth running on the road it is building.
